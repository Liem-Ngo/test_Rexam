What is the derivative of \(f(x) = x^{3} e^{2.6x}\), evaluated at \(x = 0.64\)?

Using the product rule for \(f(x) = g(x) \cdot h(x)\), where \(g(x) := x^{3}\) and \(h(x) := e^{2.6x}\), we obtain \[\begin{aligned} f'(x) & = & [g(x) \cdot h(x)]' = g'(x) \cdot h(x) + g(x) \cdot h'(x) \\ & = & 3 x^{3 - 1} \cdot e^{2.6x} + x^{3} \cdot e^{2.6x} \cdot 2.6 \\ & = & e^{2.6x} \cdot(3 x^2 + 2.6 x^{3}) \\ & = & e^{2.6x} \cdot x^2 \cdot (3 + 2.6x). \end{aligned}\] Evaluated at \(x = 0.64\), the answer is \[e^{2.6\cdot 0.64} \cdot 0.64^2 \cdot (3 + 2.6\cdot 0.64) = 10.087522.\] Thus, rounded to two digits we have \(f'(0.64) = 10.09\).

What is the derivative of \(f(x) = x^{5} e^{2.1x}\), evaluated at \(x = 0.65\)?

Using the product rule for \(f(x) = g(x) \cdot h(x)\), where \(g(x) := x^{5}\) and \(h(x) := e^{2.1x}\), we obtain \[\begin{aligned} f'(x) & = & [g(x) \cdot h(x)]' = g'(x) \cdot h(x) + g(x) \cdot h'(x) \\ & = & 5 x^{5 - 1} \cdot e^{2.1x} + x^{5} \cdot e^{2.1x} \cdot 2.1 \\ & = & e^{2.1x} \cdot(5 x^4 + 2.1 x^{5}) \\ & = & e^{2.1x} \cdot x^4 \cdot (5 + 2.1x). \end{aligned}\] Evaluated at \(x = 0.65\), the answer is \[e^{2.1\cdot 0.65} \cdot 0.65^4 \cdot (5 + 2.1\cdot 0.65) = 4.449014.\] Thus, rounded to two digits we have \(f'(0.65) = 4.45\).

What is the derivative of \(f(x) = x^{2} e^{3.2x}\), evaluated at \(x = 0.52\)?

Using the product rule for \(f(x) = g(x) \cdot h(x)\), where \(g(x) := x^{2}\) and \(h(x) := e^{3.2x}\), we obtain \[\begin{aligned} f'(x) & = & [g(x) \cdot h(x)]' = g'(x) \cdot h(x) + g(x) \cdot h'(x) \\ & = & 2 x^{2 - 1} \cdot e^{3.2x} + x^{2} \cdot e^{3.2x} \cdot 3.2 \\ & = & e^{3.2x} \cdot(2 x^1 + 3.2 x^{2}) \\ & = & e^{3.2x} \cdot x^1 \cdot (2 + 3.2x). \end{aligned}\] Evaluated at \(x = 0.52\), the answer is \[e^{3.2\cdot 0.52} \cdot 0.52^1 \cdot (2 + 3.2\cdot 0.52) = 10.060622.\] Thus, rounded to two digits we have \(f'(0.52) = 10.06\).
